Background: Food Insecurity at Texas A&M

Food insecurity, defined as limited or uncertain access to adequate food, is a growing concern among college students nationwide. At Texas A&M, research has shown that nearly 1 in 2 undergraduates experiences some level of food insecurity during the semester. This includes skipping meals, worrying about running out of food, or not being able to afford balanced meals.

According to the 2021 SERU Report, 38% of TAMU undergraduates reported being worried their food would run out, and 19% couldn’t afford to eat balanced meals. This is a significant issue that can impact academic performance, mental health, and overall well-being.

As of 2024, Texas A&M's Student Division of Affairs Office will recieve $1.1 million for the next 10 years to combat food insecurity. The univeristy has pledged to allocate $50,000 to the pocket pantries program which will ensure the pantries remain stocked year-round. This funding will increase resources for all food accessibility programs on campus as well as support new projects.

Why Pocket Pantries Matter

The Pocket Pantry program provides temporary relief to Texas A&M students experiencing food insecurity by offering immediate access to non-perishable food and toiletries, while also directing them to additional on-campus resources.

  • They reduce stigma by providing easy, self-serve access to food and toiletries.
  • They are located in familiar academic or student support buildings, reducing travel burden.
  • They help support academic success, mental health, and student retention.

There are currently 29 Pocket Pantry locations distributed across campus. With annual funding, these locations remain stocked throughout the academic year to support students in need.

How to recognize a Pocket Pantry:

This symbol can be found inside of TAMU Campus buildings that house a pocket pantry, usually pointing the student toward the appropriate room/floor.

This project also analyzes pantry accessibility by computing the walking distance from each building to the nearest pantry, assigning a score from 10 to 100 to measure how easily students can access these resources.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What are Pocket Pantries?

    Small food pantries on campus that provide emergency support for students experiencing food insecurity.

    Who can use the Pocket Pantries?

    All TAMU students, staff, and military-affiliated individuals depending on the location.

    When do the Pocket Pantries restock?

    Each pantry gets two bulk restocks (Fall and Spring) and can request up to two more restocks during the academic year.

    Who runs the Pocket Pantries?

    The program is led by Student Assistance Services and various student organizations, with coordinators at each location.
